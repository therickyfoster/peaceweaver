## üåç ECOLOGICAL MONITORING & BIODIVERSITY

### 1. Movebank Wildlife Tracking
**URL**: https://www.movebank.org/  
**What It Does**: Global platform tracking animal movements with 6.1 billion location records. Essential for understanding migration patterns and climate impacts on biodiversity.  
**Who Uses It**: Researchers, conservationists, policymakers  
**Key Feature**: Real-time data feeds, MoveApps for advanced analysis  
**Used In**: 2024 Eurasian-African Bird Migration Atlas (300 species, 3 continents)  
**Cost**: Free for researchers, institutional subscriptions available

### 2. IBAT - Integrated Biodiversity Assessment Tool
**URL**: https://www.ibat-alliance.org/  
**What It Does**: World's most authoritative biodiversity data informed by hundreds of scientists. Access IUCN Red List, World Database on Protected Areas, Key Biodiversity Areas.  
**Who Uses It**: Corporations, governments, NGOs, investors  
**Key Feature**: Risk screening for business operations and investments  
**Funding Model**: Subscription fees invested into maintaining global datasets  
**Standards**: Tracks Global Biodiversity Framework and SDG progress

### 3. Galago by Ramboll
**URL**: https://www.ramboll.com/galago  
**What It Does**: AI-powered biomonitoring using satellite imagery and drones. High-resolution, real-time biodiversity mapping.  
**Who Uses It**: Government agencies, infrastructure developers  
**Key Feature**: Geospatial intelligence with AI algorithms  
**Used By**: National Highways UK for habitat data generation  
**Capabilities**: Comprehensive Earth surface data coverage, individual species tracking

### 4. Farmonaut Ecological Surveys Platform
**URL**: https://farmonaut.com/  
**What It Does**: Democratized satellite-driven ecological monitoring with AI. Over 92% accuracy in vegetation classification.  
**Who Uses It**: Farmers, agribusinesses, researchers, governments  
**Key Features**: 
- Carbon footprinting
- Biodiversity tracking
- Blockchain-enabled traceability
- Jeevn AI Advisory System
- Large-scale farm management
**Cost**: Accessible pricing model for smallholders  
**Innovation**: Makes satellite tech available to resource-limited users

### 5. EcoSecrets by Natural Solutions
**URL**: https://www.natural-solutions.world/  
**What It Does**: Autonomous sensor monitoring for non-intrusive biodiversity data. Continuous wildlife observation using acoustic sensors and camera traps.  
**Who Uses It**: National parks, conservation areas, research projects  
**Key Features**: 
- No field operator disturbance
- Acoustic sensors + camera traps
- Mobile app + web platform
- Open data, open source
**Technology**: IoT sensors for continuous ecosystem monitoring

**Who Uses It**: UN mediators, conflict resolution practitioners

- Tools for conflict analysis
- Social media monitoring guidance
- Negotiating ICT conduct provisions
- Digital verification mechanisms
**Resources**: Practice notes, case studies, toolkits

**Example Project**: BrailleRAP for inclusive peacebuilding  
**Organizers**: Build Up, Climate Conflict Action, #defyhatenow

## üí™ COMMUNITY RESILIENCE MEASUREMENT

### 10. US Census Bureau Community Resilience Estimates (CRE)
**URL**: https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/community-resilience-estimates.html  
**What It Does**: Measures social vulnerability to disasters at state, county, and census tract levels.  
**Who Uses It**: Local governments, emergency managers, planners  
**Key Features**:
- Interactive mapping tool (free)
- 2022 Equity Supplement with race-iterated variables
- Heat vulnerability estimates
- Puerto Rico-specific version
**Data Source**: American Community Survey + Population Estimates  
**Updates**: Annual releases with ranking tables

### 11. NIST Community Resilience Framework & Standards
**URL**: https://www.nist.gov/community-resilience  
**What It Does**: National Institute of Standards systematic review of 56 resilience frameworks. Includes annotated bibliography and Resilience Indicator Inventory.  
**Who Uses It**: Researchers, policymakers, standards developers  
**Key Publication**: SP 2300-01 (2024) - Systems Measurement Framework  
**Resources**: 
- Resilience Indicator Inventory
- Framework comparison tools
- Evidence-based validation criteria
**Value**: Establishes measurement standards for field

### 12. ARC-D Toolkit by GOAL
**URL**: https://www.goalglobal.org/  
**What It Does**: Analysis of Resilience of Communities to Disasters - 30 measures, guidebook, and online platform.  
**Who Uses It**: Humanitarian organizations, development agencies  
**Development**: 10 years of field testing and refinement  
**Design Principle**: Utility and ease-of-use for practitioners  
**Case Study**: Resilience building program in Tegucigalpa, Honduras  
**Alignment**: Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction

### 13. IFRC Community Resilience Measurement Dashboard
**URL**: https://www.ifrc.org/  
**What It Does**: Red Cross/Red Crescent tool measuring 11 resilience dimensions across communities.  
**Who Uses It**: National Red Cross/Crescent Societies, humanitarian sector  
**Three Tools**:
1. **Resilience Star**: Group discussion tool
2. **Resilience Scan**: Key informant interviews
3. **Resilience Radar**: Standard survey tool
**11 Dimensions**: DRM, health, WASH, shelter, food security, social cohesion, inclusion, economic opportunities, infrastructure, natural resources, connectedness  
**Features**: Baseline, midline, endline tracking; spider web visualizations

### 14. BRIC Index (Baseline Resilience Indicators for Communities)
**URL**: https://sc.edu/study/colleges_schools/artsandsciences/centers_and_institutes/hvri/  
**What It Does**: Measures inherent community resilience through 50 variables across 6 capitals.  
**Who Uses It**: Researchers, FEMA, state resilience offices  
**Scales Available**:
- County-level (national coverage)
- Census tract-level (downscaled version)
- Customizable for state/local contexts
**6 Capitals**: Social, economic, community capacity, institutions, infrastructure, environmental  
**Developer**: Hazards Vulnerability and Resilience Institute (HVRI), University of South Carolina  
**Integration**: Used in FEMA National Risk Index

## üß† TRAUMA-INFORMED CARE RESOURCES

### 15. SAMHSA Trauma-Informed Care Implementation Guide
**URL**: https://www.samhsa.gov/trauma-violence  
**What It Does**: Comprehensive framework from Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration. 2024 update with organizational assessment tools.  
**Who Uses It**: Healthcare systems, behavioral health providers, schools, justice systems  
**Six Core Strategies**:
1. Leadership for organizational change
2. Using data to inform practice
3. Workforce development
4. Seclusion and restraint reduction
5. Peer roles in services
6. Debriefing techniques
**Resources**: Implementation guides, training curricula, evaluation tools

### 16. NCTSN Trauma Training Platform
**URL**: https://www.nctsn.org/  
**What It Does**: National Child Traumatic Stress Network 12-module online course on trauma-informed practice.  
**Who Uses It**: Mental health practitioners, child welfare workers, educators  
**12 Modules Cover**:
- Trauma-informed assessment
- Psychoeducation
- Safety planning
- Family interventions
- Relaxation techniques
- Affect regulation
- Cognitive restructuring
- Trauma processing
- Termination rituals
**Duration**: ~4.5 hours, self-paced  
**Certification**: Certificate of completion provided

### 17. CTIPP Community Trauma-Informed Change Guide
**URL**: https://www.ctipp.org/  
**What It Does**: Center for Trauma-Informed Practice & Policy comprehensive guide for community-led, healing-centered approaches.  
**Who Uses It**: Community organizers, system change advocates  
**Key Concepts**:
- Epigenetics of intergenerational resilience
- Community as unit of change
- Prevention-oriented approaches
- Power-shifting strategies
**Innovation**: Frames trauma-informed work at community, not just individual level  
**Free Resources**: Workshop series, implementation guides

### 18. Duke CCFH Trauma-Informed Communities Project
**URL**: https://www.ccfhnc.org/programs/trauma-informed-communities-project/  
**What It Does**: Center for Child & Family Health equity-centered, community-led transformation initiative.  
**Who Uses It**: Communities in North Carolina, replicable model  
**Partners**: Duke University, CORE (Community Organizing for Racial Equity)  
**Focus Areas**:
- Decolonization frameworks
- Decentering hierarchical approaches
- Decision-making and decision-rights
- Sustainability strategies
**Funding**: NC Division of Child & Family Well-Being

### 19. One Earth Regenerative Agriculture Framework
**URL**: https://www.oneearth.org/regenerative-agriculture-and-food-systems/  
**What It Does**: Comprehensive framework for transitioning to carbon-negative agriculture with case studies and tools.  
**Who Uses It**: Farmers, policymakers, climate funders  
**Case Studies**:
- **Niger**: 5 million hectares restored in Sahel
- **Mali**: Agroforestry plots exceeding native forest biomass
- **Global**: Strategies to feed 10 billion by 2050 without ecological damage

**Innovation**: Three-pillar approach (conservation, restoration, regenerative agriculture)

**Research Focus**:
- Upscaling restoration through agricultural integration
- Farmer-managed natural regeneration
- Cost-effective restoration options

## üìã USAGE GUIDELINES

### For Practitioners
1. Start with free/low-cost tools (Census CRE, SAMHSA resources)
2. Pilot assessment tools before large implementation
3. Combine tools for comprehensive picture (e.g., BRIC + ARC-D)
4. Engage communities in tool selection and interpretation

### For Researchers
1. Use standardized frameworks for comparison (BRIC, CART)
2. Cite tools properly in publications
3. Validate emerging tools against established ones
4. Contribute to open-source platforms

### For Policymakers
1. Leverage institutional tools (NIST, Census, UN)
2. Fund community-led implementations
3. Require evidence-based approaches in grants
4. Support open data and platform interoperability

### For Community Members
1. Request tools that are accessible and relevant
2. Participate in assessments and data validation
3. Advocate for local control of data
4. Share results to build collective knowledge
